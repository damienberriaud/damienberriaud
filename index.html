Publications

Stable Dinner Party Seating Arrangements [Best Paper Award] Damien Berriaud, Andrei Constantinescu and Roger Wattenhofer. 19th Conference on Web and Internet Economics (WINE), Shanghai, China, December 2023.

A group of n agents with numerical preferences for each other are to be assigned to the n seats of a dining table. We study two natural topologies: circular (cycle) tables and panel (path) tables. For a given seating arrangement, an agent’s utility is the sum of its preference values towards its (at most two) direct neighbors. An arrangement is envy-free if no agent strictly prefers someone else’s seat, and it is stable if no two agents strictly prefer each other’s seats. We show that it is NPcomplete to decide whether an envy-free arrangement exists for both paths and cycles, even with binary preferences. In contrast, under the assumption that agents come from a bounded number of classes, for both topologies, we present polynomialtime algorithms computing envy-free and stable arrangements, working even for general preferences. Proving the hardness of computing stable arrangements seems more difficult, as even constructing unstable instances can be challenging. To this end, we propose a characterization of the existence of stable arrangements based on the number of distinct values in the preference matrix and the number of classes of agents. For two classes of agents, we show that stability can always be ensured, for both paths and cycles. For cycles, we moreover show that binary preferences with four classes of agents, as well as three-valued preferences with three classes of agents, are sufficient to prevent the existence of a stable arrangement. For paths, the latter still holds, while we argue that a path-stable arrangement always exists in the binary case under the additional constraint that agents can only swap seats when sitting at most two positions away. We moreover consider the swap dynamics and exhibit instances where they do not converge, despite a stable arrangement existing.

Learning to Bid in Repeated Karma Auctions: Towards more Efficient and Fair Resource Allocations Damien Berriaud, Ezzat Elokdaa, Devansh Jalotab, Marco Pavoneb, Florian Dörflera.

In a world of finite resources, optimal allocation problems are destined to play a very central role. When the allocation problem is repeated, karma mecha- nisms allow a population of self-interested agents to achieve fair yet socially efficient allocations. In such schemes, agents trade the immediate access to a resource for its access in the future by means of an artificial karma currency in a succession of auctions. Thus far, the problem of learning an optimal bidding strategy from the perspective of a single agent attracted little work. There are however many results in the related ad auction literature, where the currency keeps its value outside the auctions. We first study a simplified setting where karma is only issued at the start of an episode and devise a simple practical strategy that is asymptotically optimal when the competing bids are independent of an agent bidding behavior, which intuitively occurs when the population grows large. We moreover show that the learning dy- namics that arise from the simultaneous adoption of such strategies converge and that the incentive for any agent to deviate unilaterally decreases to zero as the number of agents augments. We then extend our analysis to support gains of karma throughout the episode and propose strategies with similar guarantees of performance and convergence.
